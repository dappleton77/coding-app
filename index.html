Step 3: Number and Complexity of Problems Addressed

Select the option that best matches your encounter. Examples are provided for each level.

  • 1 self-limited or minor problem (e.g., palpitations resolved with reassurance)
  • Or 2+ self-limited/minor problems
  • Or 1 stable chronic illness (e.g., controlled hypertension)
  • Or 1 acute, uncomplicated illness/injury (e.g., mild chest discomfort, likely musculoskeletal)
  • 1+ chronic illnesses with exacerbation/progression/side effects (e.g., worsening angina requiring medication adjustment)
  • Or 2+ stable chronic illnesses (e.g., hypertension and stable CAD)
  • Or 1 undiagnosed new problem with uncertain prognosis (e.g., new exertional dyspnea)
  • Or 1 acute illness with systemic symptoms (e.g., suspected pericarditis)
  • Or 1 acute complicated injury (e.g., chest trauma in patient on anticoagulants)
  • 1+ chronic illnesses with severe exacerbation/progression/side effects (e.g., heart failure with worsening dyspnea requiring urgent intervention)
  • Or 1 acute/chronic illness/injury threatening life or function (e.g., suspected acute coronary syndrome)

Please select an option to proceed.

Step 4: Amount and/or Complexity of Data Reviewed and Analyzed

Check all that apply. The level will be determined based on categories met.

  • Review of external notes from unique source
  • Review of unique test results (e.g., reviewing prior echocardiogram)
  • Ordering of unique tests (e.g., order a stress test, order a coronary CTA with contrast)
  • Assessment requiring independent historian (e.g., family input for confused patient; qualifies as Low level if selected alone)
  • Independent interpretation of tests not separately reported (e.g., personally reviewing an ECG or stress test)
  • Discussion with external provider/source (e.g., consulting cardiothoracic surgeon)
  • Low: Minimal or limited (e.g., any 2 from Category 1 or independent historian)
  • Moderate: Moderate (e.g., any 3 from Category 1, or Category 2, or Category 3)
  • High: Extensive (meet 2 of 3 categories)

Step 5: Risk of Complications and/or Morbidity or Mortality

Select the option that best matches. Examples included.

  • Minimal (e.g., lifestyle modification, over-the-counter meds)
  • Or low (e.g., prescribing low-dose aspirin)
  • Prescription drug management (e.g., initiating or adjusting statins or beta-blockers)
  • Decision for minor procedure with risk factors (e.g., cardioversion in anticoagulated patient)
  • Treatment limited by social determinants (e.g., non-compliance due to financial barriers)
  • Drug therapy needing intensive monitoring (e.g., initiating amiodarone with close follow-up)
  • Decision for major procedure with risk factors (e.g., planning cardiac catheterization in high-risk patient)
  • Decision to refer for urgent hospitalization (e.g., suspected unstable angina requiring inpatient evaluation)

Please select an option to proceed.

Step 6: Time Override (Optional)
